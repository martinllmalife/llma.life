# 3.6 Million People Just Discovered Lavender Marriages. Here's What They Got Wrong.

A TikTok video about marrying your gay best friend hit 3.6 million views.

The comments? Mostly straight women. Mostly saying some version of: where do I sign up.

And look, I get it. I genuinely do. Dating in 2026 is a participation trophy sport where everyone loses. Romance is exhausting. Your gay best friend already knows your coffee order, doesn't weaponize your insecurities, and has never once made you watch a three-hour war movie without warning you first.

So yes. The appeal is real.

But here's the thing that's getting lost in the 600,000 comments and the Whitney Leavitt memes and the Reddit threads: the version of lavender marriage that TikTok is romanticizing? It's missing the most important piece.

---

## What "Lavender Marriage" Actually Means (And Where It Came From)

Let's do the quick history. A lavender marriage is a partnership between people who aren't romantically attracted to each other -- historically, between a gay man and a straight woman, arranged so the gay partner could appear heterosexual to society and avoid persecution.

These arrangements weren't chosen. They were survival.

Rock Hudson had one. So did Tab Hunter. These were real people navigating a world that would have destroyed them if the truth came out. The "lavender" in lavender marriage wasn't cozy. It was camouflage.

That's the version Whitney Leavitt keeps denying, by the way. The closeted version. The secret version. The version where one or both people are pretending.

And she's right to deny it -- because the 2026 version of lavender marriage isn't that.

---

## The Version That's Actually Going Viral

What's trending on TikTok isn't a cover-up. It's a choice.

The new lavender marriage conversation is about two people -- often a gay man and a straight woman, but not always -- who look at each other and say: we love each other, we trust each other, we want to build a life together, and the fact that we're not sexually attracted to each other is a feature, not a bug.

No pretending. No secrets. Eyes open.

The appeal is obvious. Romantic relationships are volatile. They run on chemistry that fades, on attraction that shifts, on feelings that can turn sideways overnight. Platonic partnerships can offer something romantic ones often don't: stability that isn't dependent on whether someone still finds you attractive after a hard year.

This is real. This is valid. And it's genuinely different from hiding.

---

## Here's What the 600K Comments Are Missing

The viral version of lavender marriage sounds almost frictionless. You just... pick your best friend and build a life together and it's all very cozy and sensible.

But here's what every single one of those 600K comments is glossing over: intention requires conversation.

You can't just vibe your way into a functional intentional partnership. You have to actually talk about it.

What does financial partnership look like? What about if one of you develops romantic feelings? What happens if one of you meets someone you want to pursue romantically? What does family look like? What does co-parenting look like if that's on the table? What does this mean to your families, your faith communities, your friend groups?

These aren't romantic questions. They're structural questions. And they're the ones that will make or break an intentional partnership -- lavender or otherwise.

The difference between the historical closeted lavender marriage and the modern intentional one isn't just that one is honest and one isn't. It's that the modern version requires both people to actually know what they're agreeing to.

That conversation? A lot of those 600K commenters haven't had it. And if they built a partnership without having it, they wouldn't have a lavender marriage. They'd have a situation.

## What a Healthy Intentional Partnership Actually Requires

The LLMA community has members in all kinds of intentional partnerships -- lavender, platonic, asexual partnerships, chosen family structures that look nothing like a traditional marriage. Here's what the partnerships that work have in common:

They talked about it first. Not just once. Ongoing.

They defined what "partnership" means for them specifically. Not what it's supposed to mean. What it actually means for these two specific people.

They made agreements -- real ones, not assumptions.

They stayed open to those agreements changing as life changes.

The viral TikTok made it look easy. The reality is that an intentional partnership is actually harder to build than a romantic one in some ways -- because you can't fall back on chemistry to paper over the gaps. You have to actually build the structure.

The good news is: that structure, when you build it intentionally? It holds.
