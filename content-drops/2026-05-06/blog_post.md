Let's get right to it.

Amanda Batula and Kyle Cooke announced their separation in January. Amanda and West Wilson confirmed they were a thing on March 31. Kyle is now doing that very specific post-breakup behavior where you make your social media follows a public statement.

And Bravo? Bravo responded to all of this by handing Amanda and West their own spinoff series, "In The City," which premieres May 19th.

Kyle is not in the spinoff.

---

## Nobody Is the Villain Here. That's Actually the Problem.

I want to be clear about something: I am not here to be on anyone's team. These are real people going through a real divorce, and regardless of how much they signed up to have cameras in their faces, that part is legitimately hard.

But I am here to talk about what this storyline exposes, because it exposes something we almost never say out loud.

There is a version of a relationship that is built to be watched. And there is a version of a relationship that is built to last. And those two things are not always the same.

The Amanda-and-Kyle story was a great watch. The complementary strengths. The couple that seemed to have it figured out. The fights that resolved cleanly before the season finale. It was a real relationship, and I don't think either of them was faking it. But it was also a relationship that lived largely in public, built partly for an audience, optimized in ways that very good people in very public situations can't always help.

When that version of the relationship stopped working, the whole thing came apart. And what Bravo gave Amanda and West wasn't just a show. It was an answer to a question the audience was already asking: what's the next chapter?

Kyle's next chapter, meanwhile, is figuring out which mutuals he has to quietly unfollow.

---

## The Relationship Content Industrial Complex Is Not Your Friend

Here's the thing about watching other people's relationships for entertainment. The relationships we find most entertaining are almost never the relationships we'd actually want.

We love the drama. We love the unpredictability. We love the moment it falls apart, not because we're cruel, but because resolution is satisfying and real life almost never gives it to us this cleanly.

So we watch relationship content that is designed to be watchable. Which means it's designed to be dramatic. Which means it's designed around conflict and chemistry and the moment of rupture, not around the boring stuff that actually holds two people together over a decade.

The boring stuff. The Sunday morning where one person is annoyed and the other one just absorbs it because they've decided to. The negotiation about money that takes three separate conversations to resolve. The way someone shows up at 2 a.m. not because it makes a great storyline, but because they said they would.

None of that makes good TV. But all of that is what an actual partnership looks like.

When your relationship model is built on what you've seen performed, you end up optimizing for performance. And performance is exhausting. And exhausting things eventually stop.

---

## What the Social Media Audit Actually Means

Kyle Cooke publicly sharing who he still follows after a public divorce is uncomfortable to watch, but it's also deeply human.

After any major relationship dissolution, we audit. We go through the evidence of who people are. We look at timelines. We look at who said what and when. We track the residue of connection because we're trying to figure out what was real.

The reason this is uncomfortable to watch publicly is that it reveals the work of rebuilding after a relationship that was never quite just yours. When everything you shared was also content, the aftermath has to be content too. The unfollows are public. The new follows are noted. The absence of a person in someone else's Instagram Stories is its own story.

That's not a flaw in Kyle. That's what happens when the private and public are woven together so tightly that there's no separation when things fall apart.

---

## Build for Your Life, Not for the Algorithm

Here's my actual take, and this is the one I want you to keep.

There is nothing wrong with having a public relationship. There is nothing wrong with sharing your partnership with an audience, being aspirational, or genuinely loving someone in a way that looks good on camera.

But the relationship has to exist underneath the camera too. It has to have its own logic, its own agreements, its own reasons that have nothing to do with how it photographs.

The couples I find most interesting — the ones who actually seem to be holding up — are the ones who started by asking each other what they actually wanted from a partnership, not what it was supposed to look like from the outside. They built something specific to their lives. They talked about what loyalty meant to them before they needed it. They made agreements about money and time and what happens when it gets hard.

None of that is exciting content. It's just the stuff that holds.

If you want a relationship that performs well, that's a legitimate goal. But make sure you also have a relationship that holds.

Build for your life. The audience is not going to be there at the end of the season.
