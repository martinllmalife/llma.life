QUESTION: "Is it weird that I'm relieved when a 'perfect couple' breaks up? Like when I heard about Amanda and Kyle, my first thought was 'yeah, that makes sense.' Should I feel bad about that?"

---

ANSWER:

No, and I think that instinct is more honest than you're giving yourself credit for.

When a relationship has been heavily curated and publicly performed for years, you're not actually watching the relationship. You're watching the brand. And brands don't break up — the real people inside them eventually do. Your relief might just be your brain recognizing that the performance was always going to be unsustainable.

Here's the thing about "perfect couples" on TV (or on Instagram, or in your friend group, or in your family): we respond to them as if the performance is the relationship. We get invested. And when it ends, there's a tiny part of us that goes "oh thank god, the artifice is finally over," because watching something be performed is genuinely exhausting, even as a spectator.

That relief isn't you wishing bad things on people. It's pattern recognition. It's your gut telling you that what you were watching wasn't fully real.

The relationships I've seen actually last are the ones nobody's watching all that hard. The ones where two people designed something for themselves — not for the audience, not for the optics, not for the family Christmas table. Just for them.

That's what LLMA exists for, if you're curious. Intentional partnerships for people who'd rather build than perform.
