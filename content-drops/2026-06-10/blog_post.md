## Time Is Not the Variable

Everyone's first instinct is to chalk the Jason/Jenny split up to "they had more time to build something." 18 years, deep roots, co-parenting infrastructure, shared history. Of course they're okay.

But that's not actually what's going on. Time can make either outcome worse. Long relationships that collapse in fire — and there are plenty of them — tend to leave more wreckage, not less, because there was more invested and nothing holding the structure together.

The variable is whether you built a partnership, or whether you built a feeling.

A feeling is beautiful and real and important. It is also not load-bearing.

A partnership — one you actually designed, with explicit values, honest conversations about what you both want from the relationship, a framework for what you are to each other that goes beyond "we're in love" — that can survive the romantic component changing.

---

## What "On Great Terms" Actually Means

When two people split after 18 years and call it "on great terms," what they're usually describing (whether they'd use this language or not) is: we built more than romance.

We built friendship. We built shared commitments around our kids. We built a clear understanding of what we were trying to create together. When the romantic layer shifted, we still had a structure. The house didn't collapse because the house was more than one room.

That's LLMA's entire framework in a single real-world example.

Intentional partnership — whether it's a romantic marriage, a platonic life partnership, a lavender marriage, or some hybrid you designed for yourself — starts with a conversation that most people skip. "What are we actually building here?" Not just "do we have feelings for each other?" but "what do we want to create, what are our commitments, what does this look like when it's hard?"

Skip that conversation and you're betting everything on the feeling lasting forever.

Which, sometimes it does. But usually it changes. And then you find out what you actually built.

## The Part That Hits Different

Here's the thing I keep coming back to. Jason and Jenny have presumably moved through grief, logistics, and the actual hard work of restructuring their lives — and they've done it quietly, with dignity. That's not easy. That's the product of having built something real.

Megan Thee Stallion is talented and brilliant and has every right to expect honesty from a partner. She also, by her own account, got less than that. The pain in that isn't a failure on her part. It's a collision with someone who hadn't designed honesty into their partnership.

Two different outcomes. One root cause: whether the relationship was designed or just felt.
