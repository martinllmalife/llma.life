Question: "Is it actually possible to split from someone after years together and stay on good terms? Or is that just PR?"

---

It's possible, and Jason Biggs and Jenny Mollen are a pretty public example this week — 18 years, two kids, announcing a split that reads as genuinely calm rather than carefully worded crisis PR.

I've seen it go both ways up close. The splits that get ugly aren't always the short ones, and the graceful ones aren't always the long ones. The pattern I've noticed — and this tracks with a lot of relationship research — is whether the couple built a real partnership or just sustained a feeling.

When everything in a relationship is load-bearing on the romantic connection, and that connection changes, there's nothing left holding the structure together. You lose the relationship AND the framework around it all at once. That's when things get chaotic and painful.

When people build friendship, explicit shared values, and honest ongoing conversations about what they're creating together — those things don't disappear when the romantic component shifts. The house is still standing. You're just renovating a room.

It's not glamorous advice. It mostly comes down to: did you have the boring conversations about what your partnership actually is? Or did you assume love would work it out?

LLMA talks about this a lot — the idea that intentional partnership design, whether romantic or platonic, is what creates resilience. Not time. Not intensity. Design.

Short answer: yes, it's real. And it's not luck. It's structure.
